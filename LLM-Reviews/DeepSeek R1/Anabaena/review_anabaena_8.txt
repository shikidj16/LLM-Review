## Abstract

The impending era of interplanetary colonization, particularly missions to Mars and beyond, necessitates a paradigm shift in life support and manufacturing technologies. The prohibitive cost of launching mass from Earth's gravity well demands the development of robust, self-sustaining, and regenerative bioprocesses. Here, we review the emerging potential of the filamentous, nitrogen-fixing cyanobacterium *Anabaena* sp. PCC 7120 as a foundational chassis for such extraterrestrial biomanufacturing. We critically evaluate its inherent advantages—including oxygenic photosynthesis, atmospheric nitrogen fixation, and genetic tractability—for converting planetary resources (e.g., Martian atmospheric CO₂ and N₂) into oxygen, nutrients, and high-value bioproducts. This review synthesizes recent advances in the systems biology and synthetic biology toolkits for *Anabaena*, analyzing progress in metabolic engineering for enhanced production of life-support consumables and pharmaceuticals. We juxtapose *Anabaena* against other proposed microbial and plant-based systems, highlighting its unique suitability for closed-loop systems. Finally, we identify critical knowledge gaps concerning its adaptation to spaceflight conditions—such as altered gravity and ionizing radiation—and present a forward-looking perspective on the integration of engineered cyanobacteria into next-generation life support systems.

## 1. Introduction: The Case for Biological In-Situ Resource Utilization

The ambition for long-duration human spaceflight and the establishment of off-world habitats faces a formidable constraint: mass. Every kilogram of consumables—oxygen, water, food, and medicine—launched from Earth incurs an astronomical cost and logistical burden [1]. Chemical and physical life support systems, while reliable, are inherently non-regenerative and require periodic resupply of consumable catalysts and parts. In-situ resource utilization (ISRU), the practice of leveraging materials found at the destination, is therefore a cornerstone of sustainable exploration [2].

While ISRU concepts have largely focused on abiotic processes, such as extracting water from lunar regolith or producing oxygen from Martian atmospheric CO₂ via the MOXIE experiment [3], biological systems offer a compelling, multifunctional alternative. Photoautotrophic organisms can, in principle, create a virtuous cycle: consuming waste CO₂, producing breathable O₂, and generating edible biomass from minimal inputs [4]. However, not all photoautotrophs are created equal for this extreme application. Higher plants, though integral to long-term habitat concepts, have long growth cycles, large footprint requirements, and can be sensitive to environmental stressors [5].

This has spurred interest in microbial photoautotrophs, with cyanobacteria standing out as prime candidates. Among them, the strain *Anabaena* sp. PCC 7120 (*Nostoc* sp. PCC 7120) presents a uniquely powerful combination of traits for space biomanufacturing [6]. As a diazotrophic organism, it can fix its own nitrogen directly from the atmosphere, a capability that eliminates the need for external nitrogen fertilizer—a significant logistical advantage. Its filamentous structure and differentiation of specialized cells (heterocysts for nitrogen fixation) represent a simple, tractable form of multicellularity that can be leveraged for division-of-labor approaches in synthetic biology. This review will critically assess the journey of *Anabaena* from a model organism for cyanobacterial biology to a frontline candidate for becoming the workhorse of bio-regenerative life support systems (BLSS) in space.

## 2. The Innate Suitability of *Anabaena* for Extraterrestrial Environments

### 2.1. Metabolic Versatility and Resource Independence

The core metabolic pathways of *Anabaena* align almost perfectly with the requirements for a space-based biofactory. Its capacity for oxygenic photosynthesis enables it to utilize carbon dioxide as a sole carbon source and light as an energy source, simultaneously producing oxygen [7].

**Table 1: Core Metabolic Capabilities of *Anabaena* sp. PCC 7120 Relevant to Space Exploration**

| Metabolic Function | Inputs Required | Outputs Generated | Relevance to Space Missions |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Oxygenic Photosynthesis** | CO₂, H₂O, Light | O₂, Biomass (CH₂O)ₙ | Primary O₂ production; CO₂ scrubbing; biomass base. |
| **Diazotrophy (N₂ Fixation)** | Atmospheric N₂, ATP | NH₃ (assimilated) | Complete nitrogen independence; no fertilizer needed. |
| **Heterotrophic Metabolism** | Organic Carbon (e.g., sugars) | Biomass, CO₂ | Can consume waste organics from other system streams. |
| **Anaerobic Metabolism** | (In specific conditions) | H₂, Fermentation products | Potential for energy storage/product synthesis in dark. |

The diazotrophic capability, confined to micro-oxic heterocysts, is arguably its most significant differentiator from other cyanobacterial chassis like *Synechocystis* sp. PCC 6803. On Mars, where the atmosphere is ~96% CO₂ and ~2% N₂ [8], an engineered *Anabaena* culture could, in theory, be cultivated using directly compressed Martian gas with minimal processing, requiring only water, trace minerals, and light.

### 2.2. Robustness and Environmental Resilience

While not an extremophile *per se*, *Anabaena* exhibits a degree of robustness that is promising for controlled environment agriculture. Natural species of *Anabaena* and *Nostoc* are found in deserts, Arctic soils, and thermal springs, indicating a genetic capacity for stress tolerance [9]. Key resilience traits include:
*   **Desiccation Tolerance:** Certain strains can enter a metabolically dormant state upon drying, allowing for long-term storage and easy transport as a lyophilized culture [10].
*   **UV Radiation Resistance:** The production of sunscreen pigments like scytonemin and mycosporine-like amino acids provides inherent protection against UV-A and UV-B radiation, a significant hazard on the Martian surface [11].
*   **Cold Tolerance:** Metabolic adaptability to lower temperatures is a valuable trait given the energy constraints of maintaining warm environments in space.

It is critical to note, however, that the laboratory strain PCC 7120 may have attenuated some of these wild-type resilience traits, presenting an engineering target for space-readiness.

## 3. Advances in Genetic and Synthetic Biology Toolkits

The deployment of any microbe as a bioproduction chassis is contingent on the availability of robust genetic tools. The development of such tools for *Anabaena* has seen significant progress, though it still lags behind model heterotrophic bacteria.

### 3.1. Transformation and Conjugation

*Anabaena* is naturally transformable, allowing for the direct uptake of foreign DNA, and is also readily modified via conjugation from *E. coli* [12]. Conjugation remains the most efficient method for introducing large DNA constructs, such as entire biosynthetic pathways. The refinement of shuttle vectors, capable of replication in both *E. coli* and *Anabaena*, has been fundamental to this progress [13].

### 3.2. Promoters and Regulation

A key challenge in engineering *Anabaena* is its cellular differentiation. Expression of genes in specific cell types—particularly heterocysts—is crucial for engineering pathways that might be incompatible with photosynthesis or oxygen-sensitive. A toolkit of well-characterized promoters is emerging:
*   **Constitutive Promoters:** The *PpsbA* and *PrnpA* promoters provide strong, continuous expression in all cells [14].
*   **Heterocyst-Specific Promoters:** Promoters from genes like *PnifH* and *PhetC* drive expression exclusively in heterocysts, enabling the compartmentalization of oxygen-sensitive processes like nitrogenase function or [FeFe]-hydrogenase production [15].
*   **Inducible Systems:** The use of nickel-inducible (*PnirA*) or lactose-/IPTG-inducible systems provides temporal control over gene expression, which is vital for expressing toxic genes or dynamically regulating metabolic fluxes [16].

| Feature | *Anabaena* sp. PCC 7120 | *Synechocystis* sp. PCC 6803 | *Synechococcus* elongatus PCC 7942 |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Primary Modification Method** | Conjugation, Natural Transformation | Natural Transformation | Conjugation, Transformation |
| **Key Advantage** | Cellular Differentiation (Heterocysts) | Rapid Growth, Extensive 'Omics Data | Small Genome, Model Circadian Rhythms |

### 3.3. CRISPR-Cas and Genome Editing

The adaptation of CRISPR-Cas systems has been a game-changer for cyanobacterial engineering. In *Anabaena*, CRISPR-interference (CRISPRi) has been successfully deployed for targeted gene knockdown, allowing for functional genomics and metabolic engineering without the need for complete gene knockouts, which can be lethal in essential genes [17]. The development of efficient CRISPR-Cas9-based homologous recombination for precise gene deletions and insertions is an active area of research, promising to accelerate the engineering cycle [18].

## 4. Engineering *Anabaena* for Space Applications

### 4.1. Life Support Commodities: Oxygen and Biomass

The primary application for *Anabaena* is the continuous regeneration of atmospheric components. Engineering efforts here focus not on introducing new pathways, but on optimizing native ones. Key strategies include:
*   **Enhancing Photosynthetic Efficiency:** Redirecting cellular resources away from light-harvesting phycobilisomes (a common sink) and towards reaction centers can improve the light-to-biomass conversion efficiency under the high-light conditions anticipated in photobioreactors [19].
*   **Improving Nitrogen Fixation Rates:** The nitrogenase enzyme is inherently inefficient and oxygen-sensitive. Engineering heterocyst frequency, improving FeMo cofactor biosynthesis, or even attempting to express a more efficient, synthetic nitrogenase could boost overall system productivity and reduce the energy burden on the habitat [20].
*   **Biomass Valorization:** The biomass produced can serve as a food source, either directly as a nutritional supplement (high in protein and vitamins) or indirectly as a feed for other organisms in a multi-compartment BLSS [21]. Engineering the lipid and carbohydrate content to improve nutritional profile or suitability for downstream processing is a key goal.

### 4.2. In-Situ Production of High-Value Products

Beyond life support, *Anabaena* can be engineered as a pharmaceutical and material factory, critical for long-duration missions where resupply is impossible.
*   **Therapeutics:** Success has been demonstrated in producing a range of bioactive compounds. A landmark achievement was the stable production of a functional, secreted hepatitis B virus surface antigen in *Anabaena*, showcasing its potential as a platform for vaccine biomanufacturing [22]. Other targets include antimicrobial peptides, antioxidants, and therapeutic proteins.
*   **Biomaterials:** The natural production of sucrose and other osmolytes can be engineered for higher yields, providing a carbon source for heterotrophic microbes or a starting material for chemical synthesis [23]. There is also growing interest in engineering cyanobacteria for the production of biodegradable polymers like polyhydroxybutyrate (PHB) for 3D printing of tools and parts [24].

A significant controversy in this area is the trade-off between engineering for high-value product synthesis and maintaining robust growth for life support functions. A proposed solution is to engineer separate, dedicated strains for specific functions, or to use inducible systems to switch between "growth mode" and "production mode."

## 5. Challenges and Knowledge Gaps in Space Adaptation

Translating Earth-based laboratory success to a functional space-based system presents a suite of unique challenges that constitute major knowledge gaps.

### 5.1. Response to Spaceflight-Specific Stresses

*   **Microgravity and Altered Fluid Dynamics:** Microgravity affects buoyancy-driven convection, leading to nutrient depletion and waste accumulation around cells. It can also influence cell sedimentation, biofilm formation, and gene expression [25]. How *Anabaena's* filamentous structure and intercellular communication are affected is poorly understood.
*   **Ionizing Radiation:** Beyond UV, galactic cosmic rays and solar particle events present a significant mutagenic threat. While *Anabaena* possesses DNA repair mechanisms, its long-term genomic stability under chronic, low-dose radiation is unknown [26]. Engineering enhanced DNA repair pathways or developing radiation-shielding cultivation systems will be necessary.
*   **Closed-Loop System Integration:** *Anabaena* will not exist in isolation. Its performance in a multi-organism BLSS—for example, where its biomass is consumed by heterotrophs or its waste products are processed by other microbes—is a complex systems biology problem. Dynamic modeling and small-scale ground-based prototypes like MELiSSA (Micro-Ecological Life Support System Alternative) are essential [27].

### 5.2. Resource Acquisition and Cultivation Hardware

The "minimal processing" of Martian resources is a non-trivial engineering challenge. Martian regolith, while a potential source of minerals, may also contain perchlorates and other toxins that would need to be removed or neutralized before being used in a growth medium [28]. The design of efficient, reliable, and automated photobioreactors that can operate in partial gravity (Mars: 0.38g) with minimal maintenance is a parallel engineering frontier.

## 6. Perspective: The Roadmap to a Space-Ready *Anabaena* Chassis

The vision of a self-sustaining, *Anabaena*-powered biomanufacturing unit on Mars is audacious, but the foundational research is within reach. The next 5–10 years will be decisive in transitioning this concept from the laboratory to a validated technology. We foresee several critical trends and milestones:

1.  **The Rise of "Space-Omics":** The next few years will see a surge in proteomic, transcriptomic, and metabolomic studies of *Anabaena* and other candidate microbes exposed to simulated and actual spaceflight conditions (e.g., on the ISS). This data is indispensable for understanding the molecular basis of spaceflight adaptation and identifying key engineering targets [30].

2.  **Automation and AI-Driven Strain Optimization:** The engineering cycle will be dramatically accelerated by integrating robotic cultivation, high-throughput phenotyping, and machine learning algorithms to predict optimal gene edits. This will allow for the multiplexed optimization of complex traits like radiation resistance and productivity simultaneously [31].

3.  **Ground-Based Analog Testing and Integration:** The focus will shift from single-strain experiments to testing small, closed-loop ecosystems in ground-based analogs like the NASA HERA or ESA's Luna facility. These tests are crucial for understanding system-level dynamics, stability, and failure modes.

4.  **Regulatory and Ethical Frameworks:** As the technology matures, a parallel dialogue must establish planetary protection protocols to prevent forward contamination of Mars with Earth life, and to define the ethical boundaries of engineering biology for extraterrestrial environments [32].

The primary challenge remains one of systems integration. An engineered *Anabaena* is not a silver bullet, but a potent node in a larger, interdependent biological network. Success will depend on the collaborative efforts of synthetic biologists, environmental engineers, space systems architects, and computational modelists. If these fields can converge, the once science-fictional concept of a truly self-sustaining off-world habitat, powered by engineered biology, will move decisively into the realm of achievable reality. *Anabaena* sp. PCC 7120, with its unique blend of autonomy and engineerability, is poised to be a cornerstone of that future.
